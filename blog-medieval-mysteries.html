Medieval Mysteries: Unsolved Tales from the Middle Ages

January 18, 2024 Prof. Michael Roberts Medieval History

The Medieval period, spanning roughly from the 5th to the 15th century, is filled with fascinating mysteries that continue to puzzle historians today. From unexplained phenomena to disappeared persons, these medieval mysteries capture our imagination and challenge our understanding of history.

The Lost Colony of Greenland

One of the most intriguing mysteries of medieval times is the disappearance of the Norse colonies in Greenland. Established around 985 CE by Erik the Red, these settlements thrived for nearly 500 years before vanishing without a trace.

"The disappearance of the Greenland Norse represents one of the most intriguing cases of societal collapse in history." - Jared Diamond, environmental historian

The Dancing Plague of 1518

In July 1518, in Strasbourg, Alsace, dozens of people began dancing uncontrollably in the streets. The number grew to hundreds, and many danced themselves to exhaustion or death. The cause of this bizarre event remains unexplained.

The Voynich Manuscript

This mysterious illustrated codex is written in an unknown writing system and has defied all attempts at decipherment. Carbon-dated to the early 15th century, its purpose and content remain one of the greatest medieval mysteries.

The Children's Crusade of 1212

This peculiar historical event saw thousands of children supposedly march across Europe with the goal of peacefully converting Muslims to Christianity. The true nature of this event and the fate of many participants remain shrouded in mystery.

The Black Death's Origin

While we know the Black Death was caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, the exact origin and transmission patterns of the 14th-century pandemic that killed millions remain subjects of debate among historians.

"The Black Death fundamentally changed medieval society, yet many aspects of its spread and impact remain mysterious to this day." - Medieval historian Barbara Tuchman

The Princes in the Tower

The fate of Edward V and his younger brother Richard, Duke of York, who disappeared from the Tower of London in 1483, remains one of medieval England's greatest mysteries. Their uncle, Richard III, was long suspected of their murder, but no conclusive evidence has ever been found.

Modern Implications

These medieval mysteries continue to fascinate researchers and the public alike. They remind us that history is not just a collection of facts, but a complex tapestry of human experiences, many of which remain unexplained despite centuries of investigation.
